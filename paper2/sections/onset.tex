\section{The onset window: an honest limit}\label{sec:onset}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics{figures/fig_dse_oracle.pdf}
\caption{Coverage by days since entering the stress regime (E6b), for the
causal calibrators and the leaky oracle. The day-2 pit --- coverage near
0.76--0.80 against 0.90 nominal --- survives perfect hindsight about the
regime path: onset under-coverage is score distribution shift, not regime
misdetection.}
\label{fig:dse}
\end{figure}

Every method in this paper, at every configuration, under-covers the
second day after the market enters the stress regime. The effect is worth
a section because its persistence is informative.

We attacked the pit with every conditioning scheme the framework can
express: finer regime partitions ($K$ up to 5), freshness subgroups
(splitting stress by days since entry), explicit transition regimes, and a
forward-looking acute group driven by the option market's own term
structure (VIX9D $>$ VIX). The acute group is genuinely useful elsewhere
--- it lifts stress \emph{upper-tail} coverage to 94.8\% against the 95\%
target on the post-2012 sample --- but day-2 coverage does not move. The
oracle ablation closes the remaining escape route: a calibrator handed
smoothed full-sample regime memberships --- which \emph{knows},
noncausally, that the market has entered stress and will remain there ---
still covers only 76\% of day-2 outcomes (Figure~\ref{fig:dse}).

The mechanics are visible in the theory. A tracker with per-observation
step size $\eta$ can move its threshold by at most $\eta \, n_t$ per day;
the day-1$\to$2 shift in the standardized score distribution during spike
onsets exceeds that budget for any $\eta$ small enough to be stable in
regime interiors (Remark~\ref{rem:notclaimed}). Larger steps trade
interior coverage for onset coverage --- the adaptive aggregator, free to
make that trade, declines it, which is itself evidence that the pit is not
a tuning failure. And the option market's forward-looking prices, which do
sharpen the general stress tail, contain no incremental day-2 signal
either: whatever information would be needed to widen intervals fast
enough appears not to be in the observable state at the close of day~1.

We therefore report the onset window as a limit of the approach --- and,
we would argue, of any backward-looking calibration scheme evaluated to
date --- rather than obscuring it inside regime averages. Practitioners
consuming these intervals should treat the first two days of a regime
transition as carrying coverage materially below nominal, on the order of
75--80\% for a 90\% band. Characterizing whether this window is
fundamentally uncoverable (a lower bound) or merely uncovered (awaiting a
better signal) is, in our view, the most interesting open question the
paper raises.
