\section{Which ingredient does what}\label{sec:ablations}

The method combines pooling, regimes, offsets, and adaptivity; a reader
should know what each buys. Figure~\ref{fig:decomposition} and
Table~\ref{tab:ablations} decompose stress coverage across the reduction
grid of Section~\ref{sec:method}; the honest summary is that
\emph{pooling carries the average, regimes carry the conditional
structure}.

\paragraph{Pooling is the big lever for averages.} A single stock visits
the stress regime too rarely to calibrate itself: per-stock calibration
reaches 80.0\% stress coverage; pooling the cross-section under one
marginal tracker ($K = 1$) reaches 87.4\%. This ordering is worth stating
plainly because it is not the usual sales pitch --- the cross-section, not
the regime label, supplies most of the raw coverage repair.

\paragraph{Regimes buy the conditional structure.} What $K > 1$ adds is
not primarily the further 0.9 points of average stress coverage. It is:
(i)~\emph{transition coverage} --- day-2-of-stress coverage rises from
75.4\% ($K = 1$) to 79.7\% ($K = 4$); (ii)~\emph{conditional VaR balance}
--- $K = 1$ inverts the 5\% VaR error profile (calm 5.3\% vs stress 3.6\%
breaches) where $K = 4$ balances it (5.0/5.7); (iii)~the per-regime
guarantee of Proposition~\ref{prop:soft} exists only when regimes exist;
and (iv)~the forward-looking acute group of Section~\ref{sec:onset} is
only expressible with a regime layer. One nuance runs the other way: at
the 1\% VaR level, $K = 1$'s stress breach rate (1.2\%) is closer to
nominal than $K = 4$'s (1.9\%) --- deep-tail thresholds pay a
data-splitting cost for conditioning. We report it rather than average
over it. The choice of $K$ itself is not load-bearing: $K \in \{2, \dots,
5\}$ agree to within 0.1 points.

\paragraph{Everything else is small.} Per-stock offsets change stress
coverage by less than 0.1 points --- standardization already does the
cross-sectional aligning. The layer is forecaster-agnostic: conformalizing
HAR or LightGBM alone reproduces the pool's numbers to within 0.2 points.

\subsection{Oracle vs estimated regimes}\label{sec:oracle}

The guarantees of Section~\ref{sec:theory} condition on the calibrator's
own filtered memberships; a referee should ask what that concedes. We run
the identical calibrator under three membership sources: causal VIX bins,
the causal filtered HMM, and a deliberately leaky \emph{oracle} --- one
HMM fit on the full sample issuing smoothed probabilities
$P(\text{state}_t \mid \text{all data})$. The oracle's memberships differ
substantially from the filtered ones exactly where it matters (mean
absolute difference in stress probability of 0.38 on stress days), yet
stress coverage moves from .876 to .879 and the upper tail is unchanged.
Perfect hindsight about the regime path is worth $0.3$ points: regime
estimation error is not the binding constraint, and the transparent bins
--- as good as either HMM --- suffice. Figure~\ref{fig:dse} shows the
same comparison along the transition axis, where it also settles a
sharper question (next section).
